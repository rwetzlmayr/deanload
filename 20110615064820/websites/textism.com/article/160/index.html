Blunt Edge - 2 May 01

A number of people recommended “The Merchants of Cool,” the Frontline documentary about the state of marketing to youth in America. So I watched all 50 minutes of it.

I admire Frontline: most of the best documentaries I’ve seen have appeared on it – although I hate the way they replace the voiceovers on imported films with their in-house, ponderous actor. Here the narrator is the inescapable self-promoter Douglas Rushkoff, who slathers the piece with faux-ominous, obvious, overanalyzed aspects of the culture industry, already thoroughly thrashed apart by the lamest theoreticians of all, the doyennes of Cultural Studies (the academic equivalent of bongo-playing, patchouli-wearing, Birkenstocked flakes).

Here’s Doug on the set of Dawson’s Creek. Here’s Doug mooning with saucer eyes around Times Square. Here’s Doug nodding sagely while the VP of programming for yet another culture business outlet talks about methodology. You can feel the implicit swivel to the camera after the damning words come out.

God I detest this sort of hollow button-pushing 60 Minutes journalism. Give them enough rope, let them dig their own hole, train the camera on them long enough and they’ll tell the story you want. Right on script. Preaching to the choir, easily digested righteous indignation and ridiculously easy targets.

Good righteous friends, Rushkoff whines through his nose, there’s an abomination infecting our culture. This abomination (of which apparently you’ve never heard) compromises all that separates you and your Lib. Arts BA from the uneducated hordes.

“Today, wrestling is sheer spectacle.”

“The midriff is no more true to life than the mook”

“Real life and TV life have begun to blur.”

“Insane Clown Posse ridicule women and gays!”

On the advent of Limp Bizkit, a band that “leaves critics cold, but ignites fans,” (pure old-skool cultural elitism there) Rushkoff actually dwells on the band’s “crass” lyrics.

And not a single word of acknowledgement to Thomas Frank, who’s been writing on the matter for a decade now, from whom Rushkoff stole the ideas, tenor and the tone of “The Merchants of Cool”, (he nearly stole the title as well), without any of the verve, or to Naomi Klein, both of whose writing outshines and outruns this clumsy, wrinkle-nosed swirl of pique by a factor of thousands.

Read Commodify Your Dissent, edited by Tom Frank, for some real cultural criticism, and for solid analysis of youth marketing, which everyone but Doug Rushkoff seems to realize, hit its stride almost fifty years ago.
