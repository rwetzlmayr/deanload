Layered :: 4 MAY 01

A decade ago, when I worked a little harder at being a tangibly pretentious wanker – now I’m just sort of phoning in my wanker pretence; coasting on it, really – I used old technical manuals (for things like glaucoma testing apparatus, or field machine-gun cleaning and assembly) as notebooks, writing over the existing text with a Sharpie, stopping now and again to let a particularly beguiling illustration or passage shine through. I wouldn’t hope to recall what was recorded: intense analysis of hangovers, annotated conversations, letters never sent. Why can’t she admit we’re meant for each other, etc.

Around that time I started pretending to be a designer. In the lucky situation of having a job in publishing, I was able to study and learn while paying the rent, and it was relatively easy to ride out the long interval I now refer to as The Time of Fucking Up (~3 years), which all designers must traverse before their work should be considered street legal.

I’d always been a reader, but having worked for years in a bookstore, constantly surrounded by books and intimidated by the knowledge of the people who’d read all the books, I was fully confident that anything I had to say would be singularly unimportant. The first few thousand things I designed inevitably seemed all wrong (in retrospect, most were), but I defended them to the death, as though each project were a child to be protected, while worrying – to extend the metaphor – that, due to poor parenting, the child would grow up all wrong, to vote Canadian Alliance or sell mobile phones or listen to Sarah McLachlan.

So, in hindsight, two forces of uncertainty influenced the work: mistrust of the basic message, and an urge to obscure anything that might be wrong by burying it in layers – text on top of text, image buried inside image, multiple messages, frames, filtering, tricks with the digital tools. It’s sort of like tap dancing to divert attention from the way you walk.

If you’d asked at the time, I would have said I did what I did because I wanted to do something different, not to be boring. But now, clicking around the web, flipping through the printed landscape, I see there’s nothing boring about clarity, at least when it comes to the arrangement of information.

The Time of Fucking Up also applies to writing, of course. Still in the thick of that.
