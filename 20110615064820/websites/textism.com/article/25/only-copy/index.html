Only Copy · 5 March 2001

Typography courses I teach begin on the first day with a short quiz, in which students are asked to draw or identify some rudimentary analphabetic marks (an ampersand, a quotation mark, an ellipsis, and so on). Also on the quiz is a chunk of remarkably badly set text, which students are asked to mark up for improvement.

The results on that first day are bad with reliable consistency. It becomes clear that these students, who have already completed three years of a communication design education, have never once taken leave from the incessant, droning mantras of “thinking outside the box” and “effectively conveying a message” and paused long enough to look at the parts of these inconveniences, these words, this content that they flow and tweak and kick around. Often students seem baffled that it should be a concern: they are, after all, going to be designers, not writers or editors.

But what irks me most is their surprise to hear of a difference between designing text to be read and text to be looked at.

So long as design instructors – plucked as they are from a corporate industry wherein making deadlines and dazzling executives have always meant more than rendering language visible – keep allowing that:

  • text is an elastic entity to be fit it into the space available
  • text is a graphic element, equal or lesser in value to illustration or columns of profit and loss
  • the buttons to be pushed within a page layout program constitute all that a designer need apply to the text being designed
  • typographic components of baldly practical value, such as small caps and text figures, are relegated to stylistic tricks, to be pulled out as a flag of “classical” or “transparent” design, or, more often, to add sham toniness

expect everything we read will continue to be jittery crap.
