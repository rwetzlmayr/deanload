Frantic Disconnect · 18 June 01

My Dad is not well. Chronic colitis has been messing with his system since Christmas, and lately he’s had trouble digesting anything at all: he’s lost four pounds since Friday. When I visited him today he looked gaunt and drawn and for the first time ever, old. But, it being Father’s Day and all, we still argued about politics.

My Dad and I have been doing a diametric political dance for decades: when he was a libertarian, I was an avid consumer fascinated by the market; when he was employed by the federal government and a union bureaucrat, I was a ridiculous, uninformed Trotskyite. Now he’s a conservative tax-reform enthusiast, and I’m an anarchist with occasional inclinations to blow shit up.

We’ve always agreed, however, on the core notion that policy should contribute to public wellbeing rather than the demands of the greedy and stupid. And today we agreed that the real comic tragedy of the tragicomedy that is Canada’s unite-the-right movement (and its laughingstock party, still as graceful and confident as a young girl trying high heels for the first time), is that its very beginnings, forged on revulsion with the damage done by the Mulroney Tories – odious fuckwits with knees the colour of rugburn – has utterly evaporated. The Canadian Alliance has become the very thing that its inventor, the egalitarian obsessive Preston Manning, set out to destroy a decade ago; just as corrupt, just as pandering to money and power, just as impotent in foreseeing public good.

But where I find the slow suicide of the Alliance funny, each cockup more entertaining than than the last, Dad feels betrayed. And, truth be told, he has been. Canada is run by Reaganite conservatives who call themselves Liberal, opposed by doppelgänger Reaganites with ambitions informed only by desire for power. There’s no opposition at all.

And we both feel frantically disconnected from the people making decisions on our behalf.
