Oceans of Text :: 7 MARCH 01

I am now a fully converted acolyte of the PHP scripting language, which, like its Linux dancing partner MySQL, is opensource, relatively easy to learn, obscenely powerful, elegantly designed, and fun. Having spent years working with pretty but sluggish databases, as well as ugly and poorly supported scripting languages, and never having considered the mammoth beasts, I’m astonished at what these tools can do. A tangible benefit of this conversion has been figuring out, at long last, the Zen of Grep.

Those raised on a diet of graphical user interfaces and applications such as, oh, Word, which ask you to ignore the man behind the curtain (everything is being taken care of), have been missing out. The real voodoo of regular expression matching has never been front and centre. This may be why command-line Unix twerps view Windows and Mac users with such contempt.

If you work with text in any substantial way, and find yourself making repeated manual changes, or cutting and pasting, in long documents, Grep searching will soon become your new best friend. In combination with script languages such as PHP or Applescript, it will part your hair.

Perhaps you’ve been faced with a text document listing hundreds of names in the following format:

Kalman, Tibor: Hungary, 1994
Vignelli, Massimo: Italy, 1988
Weingart, Wolfgang: Neptune, 1972

Which you need to be in this format:

Hungary
1994: Tibor Kalman
Italy
1988: Massimo Vignelli
Neptune
1972: Wolfgang Weingart

All sorts of longhanded workarounds could be tried in a word processor or a spread sheet, but likely there’d be a lot of cutting and pasting going on. With a Grep search, thousands of the above changes can be made in seconds. A good thing, Jack.

Examples, including some hidden voodoo to be found in Word, are forthcoming.
