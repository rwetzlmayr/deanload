Orienteering :: 15 SEPTEMBER 01

Where I lived before, finding one’s bearings was simple: to the West was an ocean (well, a bracelet of small islands populated by loonies and retired Albertans, then a large island of anglophiles and heroin merchants, then an ocean). To the North stood a huge smear of mountains; to the East, so the rumour went, lay the rest of Canada, and, an hour by car due South, there was America: cheap booze and cigarettes, bars that never close and, for two weeks in 1992, the salad days of Grunge.

Where I live now, car travel means leaving behind a booming metropolis of 6oo to negotiate a superfine latticework of one-lane roads, all of which will take one eventually to one’s destination, though none of which actually offer any indication of same (road signs are terribly polite but utterly devoid of information, and last week as I stared baffled at the road atlas herself laughed and said consulting a map was the surest way to get lost, doofus).

On the road returning from Alès, for example, a sign points right and says Pompignan, 9 km. Straightforward enough, but, hon-hon, ceetee boy, you see what you have to do is turn right, go ten feet, do a U-turn, cross back over the road you just left, and go nine kilometres in the opposite direction.

It’s not entirely outside the arena of the possible that I’ll figure this out someday.

May I present my card?
