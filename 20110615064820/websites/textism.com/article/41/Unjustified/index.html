Unjustified · 11 March 2001

Here’s a thought. Conversation is likely to fail when one participant presumes to define the expectations of the other. We look to the conventions of language and discourse, to an understanding of what others expect, and make compromises to the context of the conversation. Otherwise, we risk hollering into a void, never learning or informing, like Diane Francis.

Designing material intended to be read is a matter of constant compromise, of making allowances to conventions, of deciding which ideals may be sacrificed to the demands of context. The physiognomies of reading operate within boundaries: overly long lines are tiresome to read; bright white paper that shrieks behind words is irritating; loose, inconsistent wordspacing turns a writer’s line of thought to a minefield of distractions.

The tactile, quaintly analog world of ink on paper has evolved to be relatively forgiving of these compromises, even those so potentially ruinous as the stylistic whims of graphic designers.

Computer monitors, being far less forgiving, are the last place to push the boundaries of reading logic, to invoke tricks that ignore hard-won conventions.

Like, for example, justifying text on a web page.
