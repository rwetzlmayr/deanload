High Digital :: 20 MARCH 01

The Whitney Museum’s forthcoming exhibition BitStreams, “a provocative and stimulating presentation of contemporary art that harnesses digital media to achieve new dimensions of artistic expression through the transformation of images, space, data, and sound,” looks like bollocks incarnate. Might as well mount a show called NewLanes (“a provocative collection of automobile driving techniques, capturing the essence of movement in space”), or one called ChannelPath (“a striking survey of television remote usage, featuring a live performance piece by an accountant named Dave”).

And, one must ask, what good is bollocks without a baffling, eye-crossing, rage-inducing Flash site in tow.

Translucent Shopping Bags :: 18 MARCH 01

I’m reading Evelyn Lau’s latest chunk of autobiography, Inside Out, girding up for the inevitable gruesome details of bonking W.P. Kinsella.

The package: one of those twee little hardcovers that women with ironically nerdy glasses buy to augment the Real Simple décor of their lives. The design apes Chip Kidd’s two-layered faux-vellum-over-PLC cardigan for Disclosure by Michael Crichton. Here the vellum carries half the title (Inside) and the inner cover carries the other half (Out). Get it?

Meh.

I always take the cardigan off when reading hardcovers, and thus I’m now reading a book called Out.

Far more appropriate to Lau’s writing – which, I must say, rings clear as a bell notwithstanding the subject matter – was the cover for her collection Fresh Girls, as far as I know the first Canadian literary effort sent out into the market wearing a beaver shot on the front.

Inside, Goudy Old Style with fake small caps, comb-over leading and Douglas-fir em dashes with spaces on either side. Nothing against Goudy, other than toward its tendency to make everything read like it was written by Lewis Lapham. But alongside Bembo display type, it’s kind of like scotch on breakfast cereal. Not the ideal environment in which to read passages like:

I would wipe off the vomit that had spattered my arms and feel as energized as someone who had just woken from a nap or returned from an invigorating jog around the block.

Go Deep :: 13 MARCH 01

Wedgie Fulford casts a dubious eye over Planet Bruce Mau:

He’s a talented designer who now stands before us costumed as a philosopher, a social critic and an artist. In these roles he appears to have nothing to say.

And gets to the heart of the matter:

Life Style is expertly designed [sic] in most ways, but the images tend to be repetitive, and sometimes it falls into the kind of art-director errors in readability that drive readers crazy, like printing text in white on coloured backgrounds (at least it’s his own text, so maybe he feels entitled to make it inaccessible to the rest of us).

Even a Stopped Clock :: 4 MARCH 01

...tells the correct time twice a day. David Carson, a corporate designer who had some success a few years ago, is apparently still at it, still wearing the dumb smile of someone who can’t believe he’s getting away with this shit.

On editing snapshots for a new coffee table valentine to himself: ‘...and at some point a slide fell on top of another slide; and I thought, “Wow, that’s nice.”’

And, without a trace of irony: ‘We’ve just released the second edition of The End of Print.’

Look at Me :: 3 MARCH 01

Standing today before ambitious communication design students, I found myself veering away from the usual disjointed bushwa about visible language and maps of the mind and centres that cannot hold toward a reiteration of thoughts from an article I read awhile ago, by that bright fellow Zeldman.

It would be interesting to establish the point in history – no doubt somewhere in the ad-soaked marketing-mad Sixties – when function lost its lustre, when writing became secondary to its presentation, when commercial artists became graphic designers and the cult of haughty ego took hold.
